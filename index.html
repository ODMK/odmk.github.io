Direct Digital Synthesis (DDS)


Direct Digital Synthesis (DDS), or Numerically controlled Oscillators (NCO), is a method of generating discrete real or complex sinusoid waveforms. The basic method is to use a digital integrator (accumulator) that generates a phase increment (or skipping value) used to address a look-up table. A single cycle of a sinusoid is stored in the look-up table so that the phase increment skips through samples of the sinusoid at a certain period thereby generating an output sinusoid waveform at a desired frequency. Typical implementations of DDS allow for the user to set the desired output frequency, adjust the sinusoid phase, and include various techniques to reduce noise and thereby increase the spurious free dynamic range (SFDR). The internal parameters of the DDS design, such as the accumulator bit-depth, table depth, and interpolation can also be adjusted to control the frequency resolution and dynamic range of the output waveform. The following diagrams and equations define the basic structure and operations of Direct Digital Synthesis.

The DDS output frequency:


F_{out} = \frac{\left ( \Delta \theta *F_{clk} \right )}{2^{n_{acc}}}Hz


The DDS phase increment value:


\Delta \theta = \frac{\left ( F_{out} *2^{n_{acc}} \right )}{F_{clk}}


The DDS frequency resolution:


\Delta f = \frac{F_{clk}}{2^{n_{acc}}}Hz


The number of bits of Phase Accumulator for the required frequency resolution:


n_{acc} = \left \lceil log_{2}\left (\frac{F_{clk}}{\Delta f} \right ) \right \rceil

Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)


precursor - continuous Fourier Transform / DTFT



The continuous time Fourier Transform (FT) of an infinite length sequence x(t):
for a continuous-time function, x(t):


X(f) = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }x(t)e^{-j2\pi ft}dt


The continuous time insverse Fourier Transform (iFT) of an infinite length sequence x(t):


x(t) = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }X(f)e^{j2\pi ft}df


FT <=> iFT for an infinite length sequence x(t):


X(f) = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }x(t)e^{-j2\pi ft}dt \Leftrightarrow x(t) = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }X(f)e^{j2\pi ft}df


The Discrete Time Fourier Transform (DTFT)
for discrete moments in time: t = nT, where T is the sampling period (in seconds), and fs = 1/T is the sampling rate (samples per second):


X(\omega ) = \sum_{-\infty }^{\infty }x(n)e^{-j\omega n}, \omega = 2\pi \frac{f}{f_{s}}


DFT & inverse DFT (iDFT) equations:


The DFT for a finite length sequence x(n), of length N:


X(k) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{-j2\pi nk/N}


The inverse DFT for a finite length sequence x(k), of length N:


x(n) = 1/N\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}x(k)e^{j2\pi nk/N}


DFT <=> iDFT for a finite length sequence x(k), of length N:


X(f) = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }x(t)e^{-j2\pi ft}dt \Leftrightarrow x(t) = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }X(f)e^{j2\pi ft}df

Discrete Fourier Transform Properties


DTFT / DFT Properties



DTFT Linearity:



The linearity of the DTFT


\alpha x_{1} + \beta x_{2} \Leftrightarrow \alpha X_{1} + \beta X_{2}

or:




DTFT(\alpha x_{1} + \beta x_{2}) = \alpha\cdot DTFT(x_{1}) + \beta \cdot DTFT(x_{2})


DFT Completeness:



The discrete Fourier transform is an invertible, linear transformation


\boldsymbol{F} : \mathbb{C}^{N} \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^{N}


DFT Orthogonality Relationship:



The vectors u(k) form an orthogonal basis over a set of N-Dimensional complex vectors.
orthogonal basis:


u_{k} = \left [ e^{j2\pi nk/N} \mid _{n=0,1,2,...N-1} \right ]^{T}


A set of N-Dimensional Complex Vectors demonstrating orthogonality
From e^(j*0)=1, the summation = N (1+1+...+=N) when k = k', zero otherwise:


u_{k}^{T}u_{k'}^{*} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\left ( e^{j2\pi n(k)/N} \right )\left ( e^{j2\pi n(-k')/N} \right ) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}e^{j2\pi n(k-k')/N} = N_{\delta kk'}


Kronecker Delta:


_{\delta kk'}


Where N(dkk') is the Kronecker Delta, with the following definition


N_{\delta kk'} \rightarrow def: \begin{bmatrix} 1+1+...+=N, for (k = k')\\ o, otherwise \end{bmatrix}


DFT Symmetry



For real-input DFT (or FFT), with imaginary components = 0, the DFT obeys symmetry:


X(k) = X^{*}(N-k) \Leftrightarrow X(N-k) = X^{*}(k)


The real-input symmetrical DFT equation can then be written as:


X(k) = X^{*}(N-k) \Leftrightarrow X(N-k) = X^{*}(k)


Parseval's Theory


Parseval's theorem states that the sum (or integral) of the square of a function is equal to the sum (or integral) of
the square of its transform. Parseval's theorem is based on the unitary property of the Fourier transform

Parseval's Theory for continuous Fourier signals:
The interpretation of this form of the theorem is that the total energy contained in a waveform x(t) summed across all of time t is equal to the total energy of the waveform's Fourier Transform X(f) summed across all of its frequency components f


\int_{-\infty }^{\infty }\left | x(t) \right |^{2}dt = \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }\left | X(f) \right |^{2}df


Parseval's Theory for Discrete time transform signals
phi represents the angular frequency (in radians per sample) of x


\sum_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }\left | x(n) \right |^{2} = \frac{1}{2\pi }\int_{-\pi }^{\pi }\left | X(e^{i\phi }) \right |^{2}d\phi


Parseval's Theory for squared Discrete Fourier Transform


\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\left | x(n) \right |^{2} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\left | X(k) \right |^{2}


Parseval's Theory for conjugate Discrete Fourier Transforms


\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)y^{*}(n) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}X(k)Y^{*}(k)

Sliding DFT (SDFT)


The Sliding DFT algorithm:


The SDFT algorithm computes a new DFT frame each sample, 'sliding' along the input signal one sample at a time. A single DFT (FFT) is computed at the start of the sequence. Then, for each new sample a new DFT frame is computed by performing the following steps: for each frequency bin, add the new sample, subtract the sample that has fallen off the analysis window, and multiply by a complex value (rotation)

DFT starting at time t:


X_{t}(k) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n+t)e^{-j2\pi nk/N}


DFT at time t+1, eq1:


X_{t+1}(k) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n+t+1)e^{-j2\pi nk/N} = \sum_{n=1}^{N}x(n+t)e^{-j2\pi (n-1)k/N}


SDFT:


X_{t+1}(k) = \left ( \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n+t)e^{-j2\pi nk/N} - x_{t} + x_{t+N} \right )e^{j2\pi k/N}


SDFT (compact notation):


X_{t+1}(k) = \left ( X_{t}(k) - x_{t} + x_{t+N} \right )e^{j2\pi k/N}

Then, for each new sample a new DFT frame is computed by performing the following steps: for each frequency bin, add the new sample, subtract the sample that has fallen off the analysis window, and multiply by a complex value (rotation)

X_{t+1}(k) = \left ( \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n+t)e^{-j2\pi nk/N} - x_{t} + x_{t+N} \right )e^{j2\pi k/N}


SDFT (compact notation):


X_{t+1}(k) = \left ( X_{t}(k) - x_{t} + x_{t+N} \right )e^{j2\pi k/N}

PHASE VOCODER


Time-scale Modification Stage:


Background - instantaneous phase/frequency definitions


Any input signal x(t) can be considered as a sum of I(t) sinusoids with time-varying amplitudes A_sub_i(t), instantaneous phases phi_subi(t), and instantaneous frequencies omega_subi(t):


x(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{I(t)}A_{i}(t)e^{j\phi _{i}(t)}

Analysis Instantaneous Phase of the i-th sinusoid at time t (phi_subi(0) is an arbitrary instantaneous phase):


\phi _{i}(t) = \phi _{i}(0) + \int_{0}^{t}\omega _{i}(\tau )d\tau

Analysis Instantaneous Frequency of the i-th sinusoid at time t (from above eqn):


\omega_{i}(t)



Basic Time-Scale Modifications in the frequency domain


Sinusoid Amplitudes (magnitudes) typically unchanged between Analysis and Synthesis, shown by:


\left | Y(t_{s}^{u},\Omega _{k}) \right | = \left | X(t_{a}^{u},\Omega _{k}) \right |


Modification of the time-instants by constant factor alpha:


t_{s}^{u} = \alpha t_{s}^{u}


Ideal Synthesis Phase (of time-scaled sinusoid i), eqn1:


\phi _{s}(t_{s}^{u}) = \phi _{s}(0) + \int_{0}^{t_{s}^{u}}\omega _{i}(\tau /\alpha )d\tau


Ideal Synthesis Phase (of time-scaled sinusoid i), eqn2:


\phi _{s}(t_{s}^{u}) = \phi _{s}(0) + \alpha \int_{0}^{t_{a}^{u}}\omega _{i}(\tau )d\tau


Ideal Synthesis Phase (of time-scaled sinusoid i), eqn3:


\phi _{s}(t_{s}^{u}) = \phi _{s}(0) + \alpha [\phi _{i}(t_{a}^{u}) - \phi _{i}(0)]


Derivation of the Phase Propagation Equation


Instantaneous Frequency (Analysis):


\hat{\omega }_{k}(t_{a}^{u})


Heterodyned Phase Increment:


\triangle\Phi _{k}^{u} = \angle X(t_{a}^{u},\Omega _{k}) - \angle X(t_{a}^{u-1},\Omega _{k}) - R_{a}\Omega _{k}


Principal Determination (normalize the phase increment to be within the range of +/- pi):
Typically this involves simply using the atan2 (argument) function, but care must be taken to ensure the phase is in the correct range


\triangle_{p}\Phi _{k}^{u}


Instantaneous Frequency Estimation (Analysis):

Acoustic Wave Equation


1D Acoustic Wave Equation


The 1D Wave Equation describes the behaviour of sound in matter in one dimension (position x)
where p is the acoustic pressure (the local deviation from ambient pressure), and c is the speed of sound:


\frac{\partial^{2} p}{\partial x^{2}} - \frac{1}{c^{2}}\frac{\partial^{2} p}{\partial t^{2}} = 0


1D Wave Equation General Solution


Provided that the speed c is a constant, not dependent on frequency (dispersionless case), then the most general solution is:


p = f(ct-x) + g(ct + x)


where f and g are any two twice-differentiable functions. This may be pictured as the superposition of two waveforms of arbitrary profile, one (f) travelling up the x-axis and the other (g) down the x-axis at the speed c.

1D Wave Equation Sinusoid Solution (sine travelling in x direction)


The particular case of a sinusoidal wave travelling in one direction is obtained by choosing either f or g to be a sinusoid, and the other to be zero, where \omega is the angular frequency of the wave and k is its wave number:


p = p_{0}sin(\omega t\mp kx)

\triangledown ^{2}p - \frac{1}{c^{2}}\frac{\partial ^{2}p}{\partial t^{2}} = 0


3D Wave Equation General Solutions


The following solutions are obtained by separation of variables in different coordinate systems.
They are phasor solutions, that is they have an implicit time-dependence factor of e^{i\omega t}:


e^{i\omega t}


definitions:


\\Time\: Dependence\: Factor\equiv e^{i\omega t} \\
Angular\: Frequency\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\: \equiv\:\omega = 2\pi f\\
Wave\: Number\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\:\: \equiv k = \omega/c


The explicit time dependence is given by:


p(r,t,k) = Real\left [ p(r,k)e^{i\omega t} \right ]

3D Wave Equation General Solution in Cartesian Coordinates:


p(r,k) = Ae^{\pm ikr}


Cylindrical Coordinates


3D Wave Equation General Solution in Cylindrical Coordinates:


p(r,k) = AH_{0}^{(1)}(kr)+BH_{0}^{(2)}(kr)


where the asymptotic approximations to the Hankel functions, when kr -> \infty , are:


H_{0}^{(1)}(kr) \simeq \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi kr}}e^{i(kr-\pi /4)}



H_{0}^{(2)}(kr) \simeq \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi kr}}e^{-i(kr-\pi /4)}


Spherical Coordinates


3D Wave Equation General Solution in Spherical Coordinates:


p(r,k) = \frac{A}{r}e^{\pm ikr}


Depending on the chosen Fourier convention, one of these represents an outward travelling wave and the other an unphysical inward travelling wave. The inward travelling solution wave is only unphysical because of the singularity that occurs at r=0; inward travelling waves do exist.
